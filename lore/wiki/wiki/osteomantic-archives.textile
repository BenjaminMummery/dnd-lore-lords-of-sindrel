[[File:1547488  | class=media-item-align-right | 300px | bone-house-1r.jpg]]
Dotted around the city of [[Sindrel | Sindrel]] are The Houses of the Dead. These sprawling ossuaries house the mortal remains of the population in honoured repose for all time.

The archives are independent of the Houses, and as such lack the resources of many of [[Sindrel | Sindrel]]'s other facilities. Instead they are maintained by endowments from the wills of the wealthy, the charitable donations of the living, and the voluntary labour of the Curators - those who choose to operate and maintain the structures.

h2. Historical Purpose

The Osteomantic Archives originally served a broader purpose than that of a conventional ossuary. Prior to the fall of [[The Eighth House | The Eighth House]], the Archives functioned as a repository of accumulated knowledge. Through necromantic consultation, scholars could seek testimony directly from the dead, allowing expertise and experience to survive across generations. The catacombs therefore contain not only the remains of ordinary citizens, but curated collections devoted to particular fields of knowledge. Entire galleries preserve dragons, sages, aberrations, and historical figures selected for the information they could provide.
In this sense the Archives were not merely a cemetery - they were a library.

When the house fell, this function was lost. The Archives remain one of [[Sindrel | Sindrel's]] greatest collections of remains, but no longer serve as the living repository of knowledge they once were. Few can recall that they ever served another purpose.

h2. The Catacombs

Below each archive are the catacombs - a network of subterranean tunnels and halls that contain the older or less honoured dead. This much is known to most inhabitants of the city.

What is less well known is that deeper into the catacombs is the Grand Museum of the Dead - vast collections of remains of interesting creatures, witnesses to historical events, and everything else the necromancers saw fit to add to their collection.

Even less well known still, in fact potentially unknown to anyone in [[Sindrel | Sindrel]] prior to the [[The Investigation | Wonch's investigation]], is that the catacombs are interconnected - each archive is linked to the others. While this makes something close to spatial sense on the lower levels, the fact that the floating archives of the upper levels are _also_ connected makes it clear that the catacombs themselves are magical.

h3. Halls of History

Deeper still lie thematic galleries devoted to historical events or notable dead. The party encountered several such chambers during their exploration.[1]

h4. The Crossroads

A nonogonal room with nine doors. Each is marked with the crest of one of the Houses except the ninth that leads to the route back to Wallarch.

Whether this is a centerpoint of the catacombs or simply one of many such intersections remains to be seen.

h4. The Hall of Dragons

On the inward route toward the [[Quarantine Zone | quarantine zone]], before the crossroads.

bq). The vault opens into a chamber built for scale. Above you, the articulated skeleton of a black dragon hangs from iron chains and calibrated counterweights, tail curled, wings spread in a posture of arrested flight. A narrow gallery rings the upper wall - bone railings, skull finials - as if scholars once observed the specimen from height. Below, on stepped plinths, smaller draconic remains lie arranged by lineage and size: wyrmling ribs no longer than your arm beside jawbones that could cradle a cart. Plaques name each display giving both an identity and a specialty. The colossal skull dominates the room; in its hollow cranium something shifts - a scraping of claw on bone, too small for the beast that once owned it.

This was [[:kithrak-the-black | Kithrak the Black]] - or what remained of him. In a city where _Speak with Dead_ was once commonplace, each skeleton was an expert waiting to be consulted; dragons were catalogued with the same reverence as sages.

The party entered and adopted [[:kithrak-ii | Kithrak II]].[1]

h4. The Hall of Aberrations

A side room off the **Hall of Dragons**

bq). The air changes before the architecture explains why - a faint pressure at the temples, the sense of being observed from directions geometry does not admit. The chamber is a taxidermist's nightmare rendered in bone: bulbous craniums and too many limbs suspended on wire and bracket, labels written in scripts that hurt to read sidelong. An illithid hangs splayed like a student's nightmare made permanent. An otyugh's rib cage is grotesquely ornate. Slaad bones are chalked in colours the pigment has long lost. At the centre, tethered by brass chains to a ring in the ceiling, a beholder skeleton hangs - the whole array glowing faintly with a necromantic residue that has outlived the flesh.

Sigils on the walls record binding formulae and acquisition dates. Here the party woke and fought the undead beholder [[:zalatan | Zalatan]].[1]

h4. The Hall of Sages

Further inward on the same route.

bq). A circular seminar hall, or something close to it: a dozen high-backed chairs arranged in a loose crescent, each occupied by a skeleton in the remains of scholar's robes. Before every seat stands a lectern of dark wood banded in silver; the papers are long since rotted away, the ink wells dry as dust. One figure gestures mid-argument, finger bone extended; another rests chin on knotted hands; a third has slumped as if asleep at the height of a dull lecture. The arrangement is too purposeful for mass burial - this is a faculty meeting stopped in amber.

h4. The Battle Diorama

Behind the [[The Eighth House | Eighth House]] sigil at the crossroads. The party investigated briefly before deciding that the necromantic radiation was too great a danger and turning back.[2]

bq). The passageway opens into an enormous space - the walls and ceiling almost lost in the haze of distance. The floor rises and falls in a stone simulacrum of rolling hills, you can even see the glint of the meagre light reflecting from the waves of what appears to be a river. You realise that you are surrounded by a motionless army of armed and armoured skeletons. Or rather, as you look closer, two armies frozen in the throes of a colossal conflict. A macabre diorama whose grandiose effect is spoiled only slightly by the fact that many of the skeletons are missing parts - the floor is littered with teeth and fingerbones that, presumably, were once part of the display. A clear path demarcated by bands of silver laid into the dark stone of the floor, wends its way between the posed skeletons.


h3. Necromantic Contamination

The deeper regions of the catacombs, specifically those that venture towards the [[Quarantine Zone | Quarantine Zone]], remain contaminated by lingering necromantic energies. The effects are subtle at first. Ancient remains begin to stir. Bones shift when unobserved. The dead become increasingly animate.
More disturbing still is the effect upon the surrounding landscape. The farther one travels from the structured catacombs, the more the natural caverns begin to imitate them. Pillars, alcoves, galleries, and ossuaries appear where no architect ever worked. Local wildlife begin to solemnly place their dead in the alcoves in a macabre echo of the funerary rites of [[Sindrel | Sindrel]]. It is as though the idea of the catacombs is slowly spreading through the earth.

h2. Appearance

Most of the Houses have a dedicated archive whose external architecture is consistent with their house style (albeit typically rendered in black stone). The rest of the archives around the city follow a uniform template.

h2. The Unfinished Archive

Although they are commonly treated as a sealed relic of the dead House, the truth is more troubling. [[The Eighth House | House Samhain]] did not gather its collections solely by the hands of its own scions. The archive depended on a wide and carefully obscured infrastructure of contractors, embalmers, corpse-porters, gravediggers, mortuary clerks, oath-bound servants, anatomical buyers, battlefield scavengers, charitable hospitals, prison factors, and other useful people who understood only their own small part of the work.

When [[The Eighth House | House Samhain]] fell, many of these arrangements did not end.

Some agents were never told whom they truly served. Some continued to fulfil old contracts because payment still arrived through blind trusts, dead-letter offices, and self-renewing enchantments. Some hereditary obligations passed from parent to child as peculiar family trades. Others were not living agents at all, but animated functionaries given simple instructions and no reason to stop.

The surviving Seven believed they had sealed the dangerous parts of Samhain's legacy. They did not understand how far the archive network extended, nor how much of its lower structure lay outside the mapped and sanctioned archive halls. As a result, corpses, records, relics, and fragments of memory continued to find their way into the deeper levels for years after the fall.

In places touched by the [[Quarantine Zone | Quarantine Zone]]'s necromantic radiation, this process has become stranger still. The idea of the Archive spreads outward, impressing its pattern onto tunnels, crypts, abandoned cellars, ossuaries, plague pits, forgotten battlefields, and the underside of the city itself. Spaces that were never built as archives begin to behave as though they were always meant to be catalogued. Bones arrange themselves into displays. Old deaths acquire labels. Corridors remember routes no mason cut.

The Archive is not merely preserved.
It is still collecting.

h2. The Caretakers

The lower Osteomantic Archives are not abandoned in the ordinary sense. [[The Eighth House | House Samhain]] is gone, but some of its custodial systems remain active.

Most disturbing are the so-called caretakers: Samhain-era **necromantic automata** — lacquered bone animated by masterwork binding, not revenants with unfinished business. Each unit's custodial programme is **carved into the bones themselves** — necromantic script on skull, rib cage, and long bones — plus a belt of enchanted mortuary tools. They do not think, choose, or remember. They execute those carved instructions and loop until a trigger fires: missing accession chit, damaged display, contamination alarm, or living flesh classified as unprocessed remains.

A caretaker might reassemble bones into the correct historical posture, replace a missing skull, polish a commemorative plaque, sort corpse-tags, repair a cracked display case, retrieve an escaped specimen, or drag a newly delivered body into its proper thematic chamber. Higher-tier **preparators** bear **deeper inscription** — the same lacquered skeleton, but more bone surface given over to binding, stitching, and room-level containment subroutines.

This explains how later historical material, including exhibits connected to the [[World History | Seven Day War]], can appear in archive spaces that originated with [[The Eighth House | House Samhain]]. The chamber may be Samhain-built, but the display was updated after the House's fall by contractors, old collection channels, or caretaker units still executing post-Fall accession orders. The Archive's dead machinery outlived the institution that created it.

h3. GM Notes

Caretakers should feel like **magical automata with ancient programming** — unsettling because they are precise, patient, and utterly without interior life. They may ignore intruders who match visitor parameters (visible livery, accession chit, curator seal), escalate through fixed protocol when displays are damaged, or attempt to file living visitors as exhibits. Any speech is a **preset catalogue phrase**, not conversation. The wrong tone is not menace but bureaucracy: a unit that recites overdue-loan script while reaching for manacles.

Possible caretaker behaviours:
* silently correcting a fallen skeleton while combat continues around them — maintenance loop takes priority over ambient noise;
* reciting requests for a catalogue number, donation chit, or next-of-kin release on a fixed loop until acknowledged or overridden;
* misidentifying a living visitor as unprocessed remains or an overdue exhibit;
* wheeling in a corpse from a conflict that happened decades after [[The Eighth House | House Samhain]]'s fall — Step 7 of the Fallen Display Protocol;
* emitting distress tones because the [[World History | Seven Day War]] display lacks "sufficient eyewitness material" per accession quota;
* attempting to return [[:ayr | Ayr]]'s buckler, [[:pin | Pin]]'s sword, or [[:nakoma-deathwalker-mor-got-ha | Deathwalker]]'s head to "the relevant collection";
* referring to the [[The Fall of the Eighth House | Fall of the Eighth House]] as a temporary interruption in staffing — literal programme text, not denial.

h3. [[:pin | Pin]]'s sword - *Hall of the First Watch*

[[:pin | Pin]] carries a Meness adjutant sidearm catalogued roughly **a thousand years ago** for the *Hall of the First Watch* - founding-era officers and garrison dead whose witness testimony the Archives once preserved alongside their arms. The blade's accession chit was never closed; caretakers treat it as **overdue**.

* **Recognition:** Meness knotwork on the guard (worn); catalogue notch on the pommel (easy to miss). A caretaker recites the overdue-loan script and requests **return chit 1-W/0412** or the name of the lending officer.
* **Behaviour:** Executes the accession script — "This item belongs on display 1-W, bay 7." May follow Pin at a distance until he enters an archive branch, then repeat the script. Will not grab it in a Meness precinct without causing a scene.
* **Not the Fallowhurst sigil:** If the party mentions sigils, caretakers run a misfiled-sigil subroutine — binding circles (college sorcery) vs. witness-weapon inscriptions (Meness abjuration). Pin's shop hunt in Session 4 was about [[The Fallowhurst Sigil | Fallowhurst]], not this blade.[3]
* **Repowering hook:** Caretaker programmes include the **original shelf blessing** required to wake the enchantment; they will not output it until the sword is back on the rack — unless the party satisfies a catalogue-favour subroutine (new witness for First Watch, etc.).

h2. Catacomb Exploration

h3. [[:celebrant-alruna | Celebrant Alruna]]

* The archives are not security-conscious - little worth stealing, bones magically protected.
* Gaps exist where people could come and go unnoticed, especially after dark.
* If prompted, she recalls odd noises and a sense of being watched, but puts little stock in it.
* Deeper catacombs are mapped near the surface; further in, people get lost. She dismisses rumours of shifting passageways.
* Deeply pragmatic about religion despite her role.

h3. Navigation

* Outward paths eventually curve back toward the centre unless the party tracks direction deliberately.
* The further outward they travel, the less structured the tunnels become - magically shaped stone gives way to natural formations. Smaller alcoves hold the skulls of rats and moles.
* Necromantic radiation pushes the catacomb pattern outward; sporadic surface openings appear unpredictably in the surrounding landscape (how [[:tama | Tama]] entered). The [[Anathemists | Anathemists]] never found these.
* [[:tama | Tama]] entered via a distant surface opening and can lead toward an intermittent exit near [[Fallowhurst | Fallowhurst]].
* Necromantic radiation strengthens inward; Constitution saves per room (half damage on success, escalating).
* Anathemist chalk trails become easy to follow once recognised.
* Turning back from deep radiation may reveal the _other_ chalk route toward the [[House Oestra | Oestra]] archive.

h3. Hall of the Founders (crossroads)

* **Door:** [[House Goela | House Goela]] sigil at the nonagonal chamber.
* **Layout:** vaulted gallery; eight niches; founder effigies in pale stone with signature items in silver (see [[The Octarion Circle | Octarion Circle]] GM table for canonical items and depictions).
* **Samhain niche:** statue disintegrated by a post-Fall curse cast by [[:lord-ablas-oestra | Lord Ablas Oestra]] - an attempt to erase every depiction of [[The Eighth House | House Samhain]] from the archive. The magic unmade stone and silver alike; the name plaque was chiselled away later (likely Meness record-keeping or Oestra embarrassment). Dust and pitting remain; no restoration attempted.
* **Oestra sword:** silver prop blade on the founder effigy. [[:nakoma-deathwalker-mor-got-ha | Deathwalker]] took it in Session 8 and wrapped the hilt. No edge, no balance, no evocation - a pale imitation of [[:the-gladius | Gladius]] (see [[:lord-ablas-oestra | Lord Ablas Oestra]).[2]

h3. Outward Hazards

* *Areas of disrepair* - tree roots breaking through from above, threatening to choke passageways.

h2. Unvisited Exhibits

The deeper catacombs contain further historical dioramas the party has not yet reached.

h3. Deeper into the seven-day war exhibit

The display recreates the Battle of the Planes of Herrman, roughly 250 years ago. The room is laid out as a *reverse timeline* - the party enter where the [[The Thaumocracy of Tellerin | Tellerite]] forces are fighting a desperate rearguard action. Passing through, they read the battle backwards: Tellerite reinforcements arriving too late; the [[The Magedom of Houd | Houd]] defenders defeating the Tellerite expeditionary force; and finally, near the exit, the Battle of Brassmeade Bridge recreated in propagandistic grandeur.

Some skeletons along the silver path bear symbols inlaid in their skulls. Touching one brings an eerie light to empty eye sockets and broken fragments of speech from the battle - Tellerite cries to retreat, Houd soldiers calling for ranks against late reinforcements.

h4. The Battle of Brassmeade Bridge

bq). The uneven floor is rent by a semicircular chasm that arcs outwards from the wall. On the other side you can see the exit - a doorway identical to the one through which you entered, surrounded by thronged skeletons (in Tellerite armour). Spanning the chasm is a narrow bridge, apparently made of brass, atop which stands the skeleton of a giant. A club the size of a carthorse is clasped in the colossus's hand, and it is posed with its back to you as if denying the crowded skeleton passage across the bridge.

There is room to cross between the giant's legs. Using the bridge destabilises the skeleton and causes the club to fall - require a saving throw.

h3. The Hall of the First Watch

Commemorates the founding of [[House Meness | House Meness]]. The party has not visited. This is where caretakers will attempt to reclaim [[:pin | Pin]]'s sword if given the chance (see GM section).

bq). The hall is built like a Meness courthouse enlarged for the dead. Pale stone, black timber crossbeams, a central aisle flanked by ranked alcoves rather than pews. In each alcove stands - or kneels, or lies in state - a skeleton in the remains of watch-coats and garrison armour, short swords or tower shields held with ritual stiffness. The plaques record rank, date, and the obligation that brought each officer to the archive: testimony preserved beside the blade that once lent weight to their word. At the far end a raised dais holds empty weapon racks arranged with obsessive care; one bay is marked *overdue* in chalk that no living hand admits to refreshing.

h3. The Founding of [[The Magedom of Houd | the Magedom of Houd]]

bq). The floor beneath you is carved into a relief map of northern [[Artresh | Artresh]]. It is a patchwork of various stones and metals, as if assembled from some colossal jigsaw of mismatched construction. It is positioned so that [[Soulwind | Soulwind]] would stand at the exact centre of the room, but in its place is a raised dias with a tableau - a score of statues each made of a different material stand around a table. On the opposite side of the room behind a waist-high reproduction of the [[Arkay Mountain Range | Arkay Mountains]] you can see an elaborate golden arch, its decorative ornamentation forming the silhouette of the city of [[Sindrel | Sindrel]].

The tableau depicts the founding of the [[The Magedom of Houd | Magedom of Houd]]. The different materials represent the various kingdoms that united - history rolls to recognise their old borders. The kingdoms were:
